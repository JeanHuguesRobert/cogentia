# Self-Contained Documents in an Interconnected Corpus

## Status

This document is a methodological note intended for the Cogentia research corpus and, more broadly, for any versioned corpus in which concepts, arguments and derived products refer to one another.

It formalizes a principle that emerged while working on **traceability of acts**: in an interconnected corpus, a document may cite, extend or transform other texts, but its main claims should remain assessable without requiring prior external reading.

This principle is intended to avoid the charge of circular self-reference.

## Internal terms used in this note

A few terms belong to the broader corpus and are briefly defined here so that the document can remain self-contained:

- **Second method**: a discipline of thinking against oneself by making objections, limits, levels of evidence and continuations explicit.
- **Cogentia pipeline**: a workflow for producing, reviewing, revising and continuing documents through human arbitration and AI-assisted critique.
- **Derived products**: public, operational, academic or campaign-facing outputs derived from a versioned source corpus without replacing it.
- **Traceability of acts**: the doctrine that meaningful acts should remain traceable, attributable, reviewable and correctable.

These definitions are deliberately minimal. They allow the reader to understand the present note without reading the full corpus first.

## Object

The working corpus of Jean Hugues Noël Robert is intentionally interconnected. A document may refer to another document; a note may extend a paper; a derived product may reuse a source document; a research text may feed a public publication; and, conversely, public critique may reveal a new methodological requirement.

This interconnection is a strength.

But it creates a risk: an external reader may feel that each document justifies itself by referring to another document within the same corpus, without providing enough internal support.

The principle of the **self-contained document** answers this risk.

## Central principle

> **A document may belong to an interconnected corpus, but its main claims should remain assessable without requiring prior external reading.**

In other words:

- cross-references may enrich;
- they may document;
- they may historicize;
- they may open continuations;
- they may show the genealogy of an idea;
- but they should not carry the central claims alone.

A reader should be able to understand what the document claims, why it claims it, what level of evidence is involved, what its limits are, and what remains to be checked, without having to reconstruct the whole corpus.

## Why this principle is necessary

In a living and versioned corpus, internal references are unavoidable. They become problematic when:

- an important claim can only be understood by reading several other texts;
- a central concept is used without minimal redefinition;
- an internal reference is used as proof when it is only an extension;
- a normative claim is presented as established simply because it appears elsewhere in the corpus;
- the reader is asked to trust the authority of the corpus rather than evaluate the argument of the document.

The risk is **documentary circularity**:

> document A refers to document B, which refers to document C, which implicitly refers back to document A.

Such circularity may be fruitful for the author, but it weakens external readability and criticability.

## Practical definition

A document is **self-contained** when:

1. its central terms are defined or minimally redefined;
2. its main claims are explicit;
3. its levels of evidence are distinguished;
4. its limits are stated;
5. its cross-references are not necessary to understand the main argument;
6. its continuations are identified as continuations;
7. its strong dependencies are acknowledged.

Self-containment does not mean isolation.

A self-contained document may fully belong to a larger corpus. It should simply avoid making that corpus indispensable for assessing its main claims.

## Claims, references and levels of evidence

A self-contained document should distinguish at least five types of statements:

| Type of statement | Function | Minimal requirement |
|---|---|---|
| Observation | Describe a fact or situation | Reference, example or sufficient justification |
| Hypothesis | Offer a plausible interpretation | Limits and objections |
| Analogy | Clarify through comparison | Do not confuse analogy with proof |
| Normative proposal | State what should be done | Ethical, political or operational justification |
| Continuation | Open future work | Do not present as established |

This distinction prevents a text from presenting as demonstrated what is still hypothetical, analogical, normative or pending verification.

## Cross-references: good and bad uses

### Good use

A cross-reference is useful when it:

- provides a longer version of an argument already summarized;
- documents the genealogy of a concept;
- allows a source to be checked;
- opens a continuation;
- points to an implementation;
- connects a derived product to its source document;
- helps a specialized reader go deeper.

### Bad use

A cross-reference becomes problematic when it:

- replaces the definition of a central concept;
- serves as the only proof of a claim;
- requires the reader to read several documents before understanding the main thesis;
- hides an argumentative weakness;
- turns the corpus into a self-validating authority;
- prevents an external reader from evaluating the text.

## Two short examples

### Bad example: cross-reference replacing the argument

Weak formulation:

> The concept of democratic AI safety is already established in [`democratic_ai_safety.md`](democratic_ai_safety.md); therefore, this document assumes it without further explanation.

Problem: the reader cannot assess the claim without reading another document. The reference replaces the argument.

Improved formulation:

> By democratic AI safety, this document means the requirement that AI systems should increase, rather than dissolve, the capacity of human communities to understand, contest, revise and govern decisions that affect them. The longer version of this argument is developed in [`democratic_ai_safety.md`](democratic_ai_safety.md).

Here, the reference enriches the argument but does not carry it alone.

### Good example: cross-reference enriching an already assessable claim

Acceptable formulation:

> An act is “engaging” when it produces, prepares or propagates a significant effect in a system of responsibility. This document uses the minimal working definition above; a more detailed operational model is developed in [`traceabilite_des_actes.md`](https://github.com/JeanHuguesRobert/barons-Mariani/blob/main/research/traceabilite_des_actes.md).

The reader can understand the claim immediately. The reference only provides a deeper version.

## Relationship to derived products

The principle of self-containment also applies to **derived products**.

A derived product may be:

- a Substack article;
- a Facebook post;
- a LinkedIn note;
- a campaign brief;
- an operational summary;
- an academic document;
- an institutional letter;
- an oral script.

Each derived product may refer to the source corpus. But it must remain understandable for its own audience.

A public article should not require prior reading of the research document.

A research document should not depend on a Facebook post.

An operational brief should not force the reader to read the entire doctrinal corpus.

## Self-containment and the second method

The principle of self-containment extends the second method.

Thinking against oneself includes asking:

- Can this document be understood by an external reader?
- Are its main claims assessable without prior adherence to the corpus?
- Do internal references enrich the argument or replace it?
- Can a serious objection be formulated from the document alone?
- Are levels of evidence explicit?
- Does the document distinguish clearly enough between what is established, proposed, assumed or still to be checked?

A self-contained document is easier to criticize.

That is a strength, not a weakness.

## Self-containment and traceability of acts

The link with traceability of acts is direct.

When a document produces a cognitive act — formulating a thesis, stabilizing a concept, making a doctrinal arbitration, publishing a claim — it should leave enough trace for that act to be understandable and criticizable.

Self-containment is a form of argumentative traceability:

- it makes visible what is asserted;
- it makes visible what is assumed;
- it makes visible what comes from elsewhere;
- it makes visible what remains to be checked;
- it enables revision, correction and continuation.

## Minimal rule

For each important document in the corpus, add a short section or paragraph answering these questions:

1. What is the object of the document?
2. What are its main claims?
3. Which notions must be understood without external reading?
4. Which related documents enrich the text without being required?
5. Which claims remain to be checked?
6. Which continuations are open?

## Self-assessment checklist

Before publishing or stabilizing an important document, ask:

| Question | Yes / No / Partial |
|---|---|
| Are the main claims explicitly stated? |  |
| Are central terms minimally defined? |  |
| Can a new reader understand the document without reading the whole corpus? |  |
| Are cross-references used to enrich rather than replace the argument? |  |
| Are observations, hypotheses, analogies and normative proposals distinguished? |  |
| Are the main limits or uncertainties stated? |  |
| Can an external reviewer criticize the document from the text itself? |  |
| Are continuations identified as future work rather than established results? |  |

This checklist is intentionally lightweight. Its purpose is not to create a bureaucratic burden, but to prevent circularity and improve criticability.

Aim for “Yes” on all items for important research documents. “Partial” is acceptable for short derived products, provided the core claim remains understandable.

## Short formulations

> **Cross-references enrich; they should not carry the central claims alone.**

Another formulation:

> **A document in the corpus may be connected to every other document, but it should still stand before a reader who arrives alone.**

## Limits

Self-containment should not create documentary inflation.

Not every document needs to redefine the whole corpus. The principle must remain proportionate:

- the more public the document is, the more self-contained it should be;
- the more central a concept is, the more it should be defined;
- the stronger a claim is, the more it should provide its own support;
- the more operational a document is, the more it should avoid excessive conceptual cross-references;
- the shorter the derived product is, the more selective its self-containment must be.

In short, self-containment is a matter of proportion, not perfection.
