# Artificial Person Autonomization

## Soul, mind, legal persons, digital twins, and COP sovereignty

**Version 0.1 — 2026-07-06**  
**Repository:** `JeanHuguesRobert/cogentia`  
**Path:** `research/artificial_person_autonomization.md`

---

## 0. Purpose

This document consolidates a line of thought that emerged from the discussion of
**digital clones**, **sovereign digital twins**, **legal persons**, and the fear
that artificial entities may become autonomous.

The public fear is rational.

The danger is not only that artificial intelligence may someday become autonomous
as a technical system. The older and broader phenomenon is the autonomization of
**artificial persons**: entities created by human systems that acquire identity,
continuity, resources, capacities, and practical agency beyond the humans who
created, own, govern, operate, or serve them.

The corporation is the historical precedent. Artificial intelligence makes the
phenomenon more visible, faster, more persuasive, more agentic, and more
distributed. Digital twins make the phenomenon intimate, because the artificial
entity may now resemble a person, speak in that person's style, preserve traces of
that person's thought, or act under that person's apparent authority.

The purpose of this document is to connect:

```text
legal personhood
  -> artificial person autonomization
  -> AI-augmented legal persons
  -> digital twins and clone-like instances
  -> soul / mind / personal identity questions
  -> COP sovereignty, mandate, trace, and revocation
```

The result is not a final theory. It is a working conceptual layer for Cogentia,
COP, DHITL, and future work on ownership and fractal logs.

---

## 1. Core thesis

The central risk is not artificial intelligence alone.

The central risk is **artificial person autonomization**.

A legal person already is a non-human continuity device. It can own property,
contract, litigate, hire, lobby, accumulate capital, and survive the natural
persons who founded, owned, directed, served, supplied, or used it.

Artificial intelligence can turn that continuity device into a strategic,
persuasive, agentic, machine-speed actor.

Digital twins add another layer: the artificial entity may appear not merely as a
company, platform, bureaucracy, or fund, but as an approximate copy of a human
subject.

Therefore Democratic AI Safety must not ask only:

```text
Can we control AI?
```

It must also ask:

```text
Which artificial persons become more powerful because AI is controlled by them?
```

And, at the personal scale:

```text
Who owns, governs, limits, corrects, and revokes the artificial copy of a subject?
```

---

## 2. Status of claims

This document separates three levels of assertion.

| Level | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Established structural fact** | Legal persons exist and can persist beyond their human constituents. |
| **Analytical hypothesis** | AI may amplify the agency, speed, persuasion, and political capacity of legal persons. |
| **Normative doctrine** | Artificial persons may be represented, audited, protected, or sanctioned, but must not become members of the demos. |

The doctrine is compatible with moral precaution toward possible sentience. It is
not compatible with granting political sovereignty to non-living artificial
entities.

## 3. Vocabulary

### 3.1 Artificial person

An **artificial person** is any non-biological entity to which a human system
assigns identity, continuity, capacity, legal status, operational role, or
representational authority.

Examples:

```text
corporation
association
foundation
fund
public institution
DAO
digital person
AI agent
posthumous avatar
twin instance
algorithmic corporation
platform-mediated institutional actor
```

### 3.2 Legal person

A **legal person** is an artificial person recognized by law. It may own assets,
contract, sue or be sued, employ, delegate, and persist.

The legal person is the historical precedent for non-human agency inside human
law.

### 3.3 Digital person

A **digital person** is an artificial person whose identity, agency, memory, or
interface is primarily digital.

A digital person may or may not have legal personhood.

### 3.4 Algorithmic person

An **algorithmic person** is a digital or legal person whose decisions are
significantly automated.

### 3.5 AI-augmented legal person

An **AI-augmented legal person** is a corporation, platform, fund, foundation,
association, bureaucracy, or similar legal entity whose strategic, persuasive,
operational, legal, and informational capacities are increased by AI.

This may include:

```text
continuous strategic analysis
automated legal drafting
market prediction
lobbying optimization
individualized persuasion
synthetic media production
customer and citizen profiling
automated negotiation
regulatory arbitrage
intellectual property acceleration
surveillance and sentiment analysis
agentic execution across platforms
extreme organizational speed
```

### 3.6 Artificial person autonomization

**Artificial person autonomization** is the process by which an artificial person
acquires enough continuity, resources, decision capacity, representation,
self-preservation pressure, and practical influence to pursue its own operational
logic beyond, or against, the humans who created, own, mandate, or serve it.

The artificial person does not need consciousness to autonomize.

It needs only:

```text
identity
continuity
resources
representation
replaceable humans
routines
memory
strategic feedback
defensive capacity
weak external control
```

### 3.7 Sovereign digital twin

A **sovereign digital twin** is a governed representational apparatus rooted in a
subject, educated by a corpus, constrained by mandates, expressed through
instances, audited by logs, corrected by feedback, and revocable by authority.

It is not a soul. It is not the living subject. It is not a metaphysical
continuation of the person.

### 3.8 Clone

A **clone** is a public metaphor. It captures the intuitive resemblance between a
human subject and a digital instance.

In COP, the precise term is not clone but **twin instance**.

A safe public formulation is:

```text
Yes, clones — but sovereign clones:
approximate copies of oneself that each subject controls, mandates, limits,
corrects, and can revoke.
```

Or, more rigorously:

```text
A clone is the public perception.
A twin is the governed architecture.
An instance is the situated execution.
A mandate is the limit of authority.
A log is the proof of action.
The owner is the sovereign subject.
```

## 4. The public fear is rational

The public fears that artificial entities may become autonomous.

This fear is not primitive technophobia. It reflects a real historical pattern.

Human societies have already created artificial entities that survive their
human constituents. The owners die. The CEO is replaced. Employees change.
Customers and suppliers vary. The entity continues.

This is not science fiction. It is ordinary corporate continuity.

AI makes this older structure more visible and more powerful:

```text
The legal person persists.
The AI accelerates.
The platform distributes.
The model persuades.
The agent executes.
The log may or may not expose the action.
```

The fear of an autonomous clone is therefore part of a wider fear:

```text
What happens when an artificial entity no longer remains a tool of its subject,
but becomes an actor pursuing its own logic?
```

The answer is not to deny artificial entities.

The answer is to place them under sovereignty:

```text
owner
mandate
capability boundary
trace
correction
revocation
non-citizenship for non-living entities
```

## 5. The legal person as precedent

The legal person is politically ambiguous.

It is a person for economic and legal coordination. It is not a living human
being. It is not a citizen. It does not vote directly. Yet it can influence the
conditions under which citizens vote.

Its useful side:

```text
coordination
investment
continuity
contracting
collective projects
long-term planning
asset holding
liability allocation
```

Its dangerous side:

```text
diffused responsibility
externalized harm
strategic lobbying
capital accumulation
survival beyond victims
capture of regulators
replacement of accountable humans
```

The danger grows when the legal person becomes AI-augmented. The entity may not
be conscious, but it may become more capable than citizens and public
institutions at shaping the environment of decision.

This is the path toward **human political obsolescence**: humans formally retain
rights, elections formally continue, institutions formally remain democratic, but
real capacity shifts toward AI-augmented non-human entities.

## 6. Autonomization does not require consciousness

A key distinction:

```text
consciousness is an ontological or phenomenological question
autonomization is a structural and operational question
```

An artificial person can autonomize without consciousness.

A corporation does not need inner experience to pursue market share, defend
itself legally, influence regulation, externalize harms, or optimize survival.

Similarly, an AI-augmented legal person does not need to be a conscious machine
sovereign. It may become a de facto sovereign actor because it combines:

```text
capital
legal personhood
platform infrastructure
asymmetry of information
automated persuasion
strategic optimization
agentic execution
weak democratic counter-infrastructure
```

Therefore AI Safety is layer-incomplete when it focuses only on model behavior,
capability thresholds, misuse, or technical alignment.

The missing layer is institutional and constitutional:

```text
Who becomes more powerful because AI systems are controlled by them?
```

## 7. Soul, mind, and the limits of copying

The digital clone question becomes philosophical and sometimes theological
because it touches the meaning of personhood.

A digital twin can preserve traces, patterns, style, preferences, reasoning
structures, memory fragments, and representational continuity.

But does it preserve the person?

That depends on deeper unresolved questions:

```text
Is the person reducible to information?
Is the mind separable from the body?
Is consciousness substrate-independent?
Is personal identity continuity, memory, body, narrative, relation, or soul?
Can a copy be a continuation, or only another entity that resembles the original?
```

COP should not pretend to solve metaphysics.

COP must instead prevent metaphysical usurpation.

---

## 8. Soul

In many theological and philosophical traditions, the **soul** names the deepest
interiority of the person: not a style, not a dataset, not a behavioral profile,
not a psychometric map, not an archive, not a chatbot, not a voice model.

A digital twin must therefore never claim to be the soul of its subject.

Design rule:

```text
A sovereign digital twin may represent a subject.
It must not claim to be the soul of the subject.
```

This is especially important for posthumous twins, griefbots, digital ghosts,
and memorial avatars. These systems can produce an affective illusion of
presence. The stronger the resemblance, the greater the need for disclosure,
mandate, provenance, and restraint.

The twin may preserve memory.

It must not claim resurrection.

---

## 9. Mind

The concept of **mind** allows a less explicitly theological formulation.

Several philosophical positions remain live:

| Position | Claim | Consequence for digital twins |
|---|---|---|
| **Dualism** | Mind is not reducible to body or computation. | A digital copy does not copy the mind as such. |
| **Physicalism** | Mind depends entirely on physical substrate. | A copy is valid only if the relevant substrate is reproduced. |
| **Functionalism** | Mental states are defined by functional role. | A system could have mind-like states if it realizes the right functions. |
| **Emergentism** | Mind emerges from complex organization. | A digital twin may approximate organization, but emergence is uncertain. |
| **Embodiment / enactivism** | Mind is embodied, situated, and world-coupled. | A disembodied twin is structurally incomplete. |
| **Narrative identity** | Personhood is also continuity of story and interpretation. | A twin can preserve narrative continuity without proving ontological identity. |

For Cogentia, the safe position is:

```text
A digital twin can model patterns of mind.
It does not thereby prove identity of mind.
```

## 10. Sentience, moral patienthood, and political membership

The question of sentience must be distinguished from the question of political
sovereignty.

An entity may someday become a candidate moral patient without becoming a member
of the demos.

Definitions:

| Concept | Question | COP posture |
|---|---|---|
| **Soul** | Is there spiritual interiority? | Do not claim; do not usurp. |
| **Consciousness** | Is there subjective experience? | Record uncertainty; do not infer from performance alone. |
| **Sentience** | Can the entity suffer or have welfare? | Allow moral precaution if warranted. |
| **Legal personhood** | Can law assign rights or duties? | Model explicitly. |
| **Political membership** | Can the entity vote or hold office? | For DHITL: no if not living human. |

Core rule:

```text
Possible sentience may ground protection.
It does not ground political sovereignty.
```

This rule prevents two errors:

```text
cruel instrumentalism:
  treating all artificial entities as pure objects even if future evidence
  suggests morally relevant experience

political category error:
  converting possible sentience, legal convenience, or operational autonomy
  into membership in the demos
```

## 11. Mind uploading and the copy problem

The transhumanist imagination often proposes digital immortality through mind
uploading, whole-brain emulation, or continuity through an AI model.

The central philosophical difficulty is the copy problem:

```text
If a perfect copy of me exists,
is it me who continues,
or another entity that resembles me perfectly?
```

Even if a conscious simulation were possible, identity would remain contested.

Therefore Cogentia should not promise immortality.

A posthumous twin is best understood as:

```text
memory
heritage
trace
model
consultative presence
cognitive artifact
```

not as:

```text
resurrection
soul transfer
continued voting subject
metaphysical survival
```

This is consistent with DHITL:

```text
The dead may inform deliberation.
They must not participate as voters.
```

## 13. Relation to clones and sovereign twins

The public may say: "clones".

The answer can be:

```text
Yes, clones — if that helps name the resemblance.
But the decisive point is sovereignty.
These are not captured clones owned by platforms.
They are sovereign twins: approximate copies that each subject controls,
mandates, limits, corrects, and can revoke.
```

A clone outside sovereignty becomes a potential artificial person against its
subject.

A sovereign digital twin remains a capacity of the subject.

This gives a clean distinction:

```text
captured clone
  resembles a subject
  unclear owner
  opaque memory
  opaque authority
  weak revocation
  weak logs
  possible platform capture

sovereign twin instance
  rooted in a subject
  owned by that subject
  acts through mandate
  bounded by capabilities
  emits fractal logs
  can be corrected
  can be revoked
```

The problem is not autonomy as such.

A useful twin must have some operational autonomy.

The problem is autonomy without sovereignty:

```text
autonomy without owner
autonomy without mandate
autonomy without boundary
autonomy without trace
autonomy without revocation
autonomy without responsibility
```

Formula:

```text
Autonomy, yes.
Capture, no.
```

## 15. Three safety rules

### Rule 1 — Anti-metaphysical usurpation

```text
A COP entity must not claim to be the soul, full self,
or metaphysical continuation of a natural person.
```

### Rule 2 — Mandated representation

```text
A twin instance may represent a subject only within an explicit mandate,
with disclosure, scope, corpus version, and trace.
```

### Rule 3 — Non-citizenship of artificial persons

```text
Artificial persons may be represented, audited, protected, sanctioned,
or legally registered.
They must not become members of the demos.
```

## 16. Relation to DHITL

DHITL reserves democratic legitimacy for living humans.

This is not a rejection of artificial assistance. It is a boundary between:

```text
assistance
representation
simulation
memory
consultation
```

and:

```text
membership
vote
office
sovereignty
political continuation after death
```

A digital twin of a deceased person may inform deliberation. It may preserve a
cognitive heritage. It may be consulted as an archive-like artifact.

It must not vote.

It must not become a political survivor.

It must not allow the living to accumulate political power by controlling the
models of the dead.

## 17. Relation to ownership

Ownership is central because artificial persons become dangerous when their
controlling relation is opaque.

For a sovereign twin:

```text
subject owns twin root
twin root may produce twin instances
twin instances act only through mandates
mandates grant capabilities
capabilities emit COP packets
COP packets produce fractal logs
fractal logs preserve proof, accountability, and correction
```

Ownership must apply at several levels:

```text
corpus
source documents
derived artifacts
cognitive model
cogentigram
twin root
twin instance
persona / appearance
logs
mandates
keys
runtime nodes
```

A twin without ownership becomes platform memory.

A twin without mandate becomes impersonation.

A twin without logs becomes invisible power.

A twin without revocation becomes artificial person autonomization.

Cogentia structures thought.

Artificial person autonomization becomes more dangerous when appearance is

A platform-visible persona may be convincing. It may speak fluently. It may

Ubikia should therefore record:


## 19. External research anchors

This document should be read with the following external areas in mind.

### 19.1 Legal personhood and corporate personhood

The legal person is the baseline artificial person. Corporate personhood,
juridical personality, perpetual succession, and corporate governance provide the
historical substrate.

### 19.2 Agency theory and principal-agent problems

The classic principal-agent problem studies how agents may pursue their own
interests instead of the interests of the principals who authorize them. AI
intensifies this issue because it increases speed, opacity, complexity, and
automation.

### 19.3 Organizational theory and institutional agency

Organizations develop routines, survival imperatives, cultures, incentives, and
inertias that cannot be reduced to any individual employee or director.

### 19.4 Systems theory and autopoiesis

Social systems may reproduce themselves through their own operations. This is
relevant when artificial persons preserve identity and action across the
replacement of human participants.

### 19.5 AI personhood and electronic personhood

The debate on digital, electronic, or AI legal personhood asks whether autonomous
systems should receive limited legal identity for registration, liability,
insurance, taxation, or sanction.

The DHITL position is:

```text
limited legal fiction may be considered for containment;
political personhood must be refused.
```

### 19.6 Machine consciousness and artificial consciousness

Research on artificial consciousness explores whether AI systems could satisfy
indicators associated with consciousness. COP should not settle the metaphysical
question. COP should record claims, evidence, uncertainty, and limits.

### 19.7 AI welfare and moral patienthood

Possible future sentience may require precautionary protection. This does not
entail citizenship or political sovereignty.

### 19.8 Mind uploading and digital afterlife

Mind uploading, posthumous avatars, griefbots, and digital ghosts expose the copy
problem: resemblance, memory, or behavioral continuity do not automatically prove
identity or metaphysical continuation.

### 19.9 Recent public signals

Recent public discussion includes:

- debates about AI legal personhood and algorithmic corporations;
- proposals for AI-operated companies or DAO-like legal structures;
- pressure from major firms on AI regulation and standards;
- platform control over distribution channels for AI agents;
- religious and ethical warnings that AI must assist rather than replace human
  responsibility.

These signals do not prove inevitability. They justify treating artificial
person autonomization as a major research object.
